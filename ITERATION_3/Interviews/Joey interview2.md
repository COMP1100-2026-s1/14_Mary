Interviewer [I]: All right, I have got the permission to record. This is the final round of interviews for COMP1100, and the interviewee is a student who has tried out our project, the minimum viable product. So first of all, can I have your name and major?

Daniel [D]: Yeah, my name is Daniel. I'm studying pharmacy.

[I]: After getting used to this product, what do you think it is trying to help you with?

[D]: Well, it helps me summarize the key information of the courses. The information is much more concise, and everything in the detailed introduction is relevant and effective. Plus, it can tell me what courses to choose in the future. That is really helpful.

[I]: yah, conciseness and future recommendations. So how useful does the idea of seeing a full course based on your degree plan feel to you?

[D]: This would be very useful advice. It saves you a lot of time searching through various resources and straight up gives you a complete plan. This is constructively helpful for first year students, and it also shows students what courses they will be studying down the line.

[I]: How valuable is it to know what future courses depend on a course as a prerequisite?

[D]: I think that's incredibly valuable. For Pharmacy, a lot of foundational courses, like introductory chemistry or pharmacology, are linked to a long chain of advanced compulsory courses later on. The official system only tells me that I need to take Course B before Course A, but it doesn't tell me that if I fail Course B, how it's going to ripple out and affect my courses in Year 3 or Year 4. Your feature allows me to see the knock on effect of a course at a single glance, which is extremely helpful for timetable planning and failing-risk warnings.

[I]: Alright, let's take a look at the course module. For now, the course reviews are presented in a scripted format. But how helpful do you think these reviews, such as comments and ratings, would be in helping you choose your courses?

[D]: It's pretty good; it's a way for students to get to know a course. But I do have a concern. For instance, if all the courses only have positive ratings, then the rating system loses its meaning. But if there are negative ratings, I might question whether the review is objective or not. It feels like the positioning of the rating mechanism is a bit vague. The comments themselves are fine; you could widely crowdsource reviews from students who have taken the course and highlight the high-quality ones. Also, a thought just crossed my mind. I wonder if the comments might just turn into a tool for students to hunt for easier courses, I don't know.

[I]: Hmm, you make a fair point. Too many positive ratings reduce their value as a reference, while negative ones make it hard to verify objectivity. We will take it into consideration afterwards. And is there any information you would expect to see here that is missing from the demo?

[D]: The core features are all there. I guess I'd also like to see the assessment breakdown for each course, like whether there's a final exam, what percentage the exam accounts for, or if there are mandatory placement hours. Also, it would be perfect if you could add a preview of the required e-textbooks for the course, along with some second-hand book links.

[I]: True, the assessment breakdown of a course is definitely a major point.

[D]: Exactly. And I also noticed that your website doesn't state whether a course is offered in Semester 1 or Semester 2. Right? I didn't seem to see it in the overview. You could also add a hyperlink to the UQ Library so students can easily inquire about related resources.

[I]: Very valuable suggestions. Okay, from what you have seen, would this help reduce the need to check multiple websites when choosing courses? Why or why not?

[D]: Absolutely. It definitely would. Before this, I had to keep multiple tabs open at the same time when picking courses: one for UQ Programs and Courses to check requirements, one for Blackboard ,learn uq, to look at past Electronic Course Profiles, ECPs, and probably browse various discussion boards to see seniors' reviews. Since you integrate all this information together in a very concise format, I really don't need to hop between different websites anymore. It saves a massive amount of time.

[I]: How would you feel about advertisements being shown while using a product like this, for example one appearing every few searches, and would that affect your willingness to use it?

[D]: That depends on whether the placement of the ads disrupts the user experience. If it's like what you said, where a full-screen ad pops up every few searches and I have to struggle to find the close button to dismiss it, that would indeed be quite annoying. The number of ads matters too; if there are too many and the layout gets cluttered, I might honestly just go back to using the official UQ course descriptions. But if it's just banner ads on the side or at the bottom of the page, or student-related ads like student accommodation, clubs, or second-hand book trading, I think it's completely acceptable, especially considering you guys need to cover the maintenance costs for running the platform.

[I]: Thank you for your coorporation. Nice to meet you.
