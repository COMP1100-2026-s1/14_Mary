Interviewer [I]: Now i am talking with Tony, a computer science student. Tony has just tried out our product, the minimum viable product. First question, After getting used to this product, what do you think it is trying to help you with?

Tony [T]: Well, from what I've experienced in the demo, it's clearly an all-in-one platform trying to simplify the whole course selection and degree planning process at UQ. Instead of having to browse through scattered pages, it extracts and condenses the absolute key information into a single clean interface, especially showing how courses connect with future semesters.

[I]: So how useful does the idea of seeing a full course based on your degree plan feel to you?

[T]: It's highly practical, especially for first-year students who are still clueless about their progression. Usually, you have to manually copy-paste courses from the official PDF into an Excel sheet to map out your semesters. This feature straight-up gives you a visual blueprint of your entire degree template, which saves a massive amount of time and prevents you from missing core requirements.

[I]: How valuable is it to know what future courses depend on a course as a prerequisite?

[T]: Honestly, this is a major game changer because the official UQ Courses website only works backwards. it only tells you what you should have taken before entering a course. I noticed that you designed the future course and prereqs in a tree, it's intuitive. And the UQ web doesn't show you the forward dependency tree, apparently. 

[I]: Alright, let's talk about rating and review. you know, it's in a scripted format till now and we will collect real feedbacks from students in the future. But how helpful do you think these reviews, such as comments and ratings, would be in helping you choose your courses?

[T]: I think adding a peer review and rating system is an absolute stroke of genius, and it's going to be incredibly helpful. Honestly, the official website entries are always so dry; they never tell you what a course is actually like. Having genuine feedback and ratings from past students right there on the platform completely removes the guesswork. knowing the actual difficulty curve, the lecture quality, and how supportive the tutors are before enrolling is pure gold.

[I]: And is there any information you would expect to see here that is missing from the demo?

[T]: The layout is great, but a couple of critical data fields are missing. First, I couldn't see the semester availability,whether a course is offered in Semester 1, Semester 2, or Summer. That's vital for planning. Second, it would be awesome to see the assessment breakdown, like the exact percentage of the Final Exam versus progressive assignments. Also, adding quick hyperlinks to the UQ Library or direct official advising contacts would make it a truly comprehensive academic portal.

[I]: Okay, from what you have seen, would this help reduce the need to check multiple websites when choosing courses? Why or why not?

[T]:Absolutely, one hundred persent. Before this, my browser would always have at least four tabs open simultaneously: one for UQ Programs and Courses, one for Blackboard to check past course profiles, another for my Student Center, and some student forums like Reddit to look up reviews. By integrating the course code lookup, structure mapping, and reviews into one screen, it completely eliminates the frustration of hopping between tabs.

[I]: How would you feel about advertisements being shown while using a product like this, for example one appearing every few searches, and would that affect your willingness to use it?

[T]:If you throw intrusive, full-screen pop-up ads every five clicks where I have to search for a tiny 'X' button to close them, it's going to ruin the experience and I might just go back to the manual official search. However, since you guys have maintenance and server costs to run this platform, static banner ads on the side or at the bottom are perfectly fine. It would be even better if the ads are relevant to us, like student accommodation, second-hand textbook trading, or university clubs.

[I]: Thank you. That's all for the interview. Nice to meet you.

[T]:Nice to meet you.
