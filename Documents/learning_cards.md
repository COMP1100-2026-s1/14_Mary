Learning Card 1 

 

We believe that UQ students have issues during the course selection process which could be amended by us using technology. 

 

We observed that 85% of the students that were interviewed had trouble with the course selection process as a direct result of how scattered the relevant information was in the current UQ resources. 

 

We have learned that to make the course selection process easier all of the information that is relevant to picking courses should be centralized in one space to eliminate potential confusion by students. 



# Learning Card 2:

* We have learned that many UQ students face inconveniences and gaps in knowledge & awareness in terms of what information UQ provides, as well as how easily accessible & understandable it is when:
    - Trying to parse the information and decide which courses to pick
    - Trying to understand the requirements & prohibitions of each course
    - Comparing courses, understanding what each one teaches/offers, and what to choose to complete their semester

* We have learned that the issues regarding the UQ enrollment system isn't just with picking courses, but with UI & features as well. There were a notable number of students who expressed that they thought that the UQ enrollment system should have come with features that were present in other enrollment systems from previous institutions they studied at, including a built-in subject details page instead of it being separate on Course Profiles, a built-in subject requirements lister, and more, that would highly expedite the enrollment process along with providing sufficient knowledge & awareness of subjects and the state of enrollment progress/requirements

* Amusingly, we have also observed that many interviewees expressed the opinion that pumping data into ChatGPT and having them produce a subject/study plan for them ironically worked better than the Course Profiles. They attributed this to both scope, accessibility and ease-of-information-parsing. They felt that relying on AI, which carries risks on its own, was better than being forced to peruse multiple different web pages to check subject details, cross-reference subject requirements and prohibitions, and then make a plan to enroll for their chosen list of subjects. This should elicit some concern as to the competency and state of UQ's current enrollment system, which should be addressed and looked into with speed.
